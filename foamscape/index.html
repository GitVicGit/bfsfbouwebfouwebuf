Foamscape

Sea foam is primarily a natural product of ocean biogeochemistry rather than pollution.It forms when biological surfactants, dissolved organic matter and wave turbulence stabilise bubbles. Foam concentrates nutrients, organic molecules, microorganisms and contaminants at the ocean–atmosphere boundary. Its composition reflects surrounding plankton communities and acts as a biochemical fingerprint of ecosystem activity.

As part of the sea-surface microlayer, sea foam influences gas exchange, heat transfer, aerosol formation and carbon cycling. It also functions as a temporary biogeochemical hotspot, accelerating microbial activity and nutrient recycling.

Human-driven eutrophication, plastic pollution and harmful algal blooms increasingly alter its chemistry and ecological role. Persistent foams can concentrate toxins and release them into the atmosphere through bursting bubbles and sea spray. Climate change further modifies foam formation through warming, acidification and stronger storms, potentially affecting climate feedbacks.

Sea foam is therefore both an active component of marine ecosystems and a sensitive indicator of global ocean health.
