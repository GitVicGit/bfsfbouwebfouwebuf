Foamscape

Foamscape

2026

Video

1min13s



In Foamscape, Victor Guerin records sea foam on Belle-Île-en-Mer, Brittany, France, as it gathers, folds and disperses.



Foam occupies an intermediate condition: seawater disrupted by air, organic matter drawn upwards and liquid structured into delicate films. Its stability depends upon surface-active compounds released by phytoplankton, algae and microorganisms. Proteins, lipids, carbohydrates and extracellular polymers gather around bubbles formed by breaking waves, delaying their collapse. Produced through the interaction of physical forces and biological processes, foam forms a transient yet concentrated biochemical environment.1 It is closely connected to the sea-surface microlayer – the ocean’s uppermost layer, typically less than one millimetre thick – which regulates exchanges of gases, heat and material between ocean and atmosphere.2


The moving image is accompanied by an analytical display that translates every frame according to brightness and colour. Dark values gather towards one region, luminous values towards another, while red, green and blue form fluctuating traces. Horizon, water and foam are reorganised as electronic values within the recorded image. This operation does not reveal the composition of the sea, but how the camera and editing system have encoded its appearance. The Atlantic becomes simultaneously landscape and dataset, appearance and measurement. Pale foam separates into intensities and chromatic variations. Resembling a scientific instrument without claiming scientific authority, the display measures the video signal rather than the proteins, plankton, pollutants or microorganisms within the water. Its function is epistemological: it makes the apparatus of observation visible.


Marine foam may contain organic molecules, bacteria, viruses, planktonic cells, minerals and anthropogenic contaminants. Research has shown that viruses can be enriched in foam and the surface microlayer, then transferred into aerosols and rain.3 As bubbles rise, compounds and particles accumulate at their air–water interfaces. At the surface, the bubbles may persist temporarily as foam or rupture, ejecting microscopic droplets into the atmosphere. These sea-spray aerosols can transport biological and chemical material from water into air, while their production and composition are affected by sea-surface temperature.4



Foamscape holds this disproportion between the visible event and the system to which it belongs. Belle-Île-en-Mer is geographically specific, yet the movements recorded there exceed the frame. Laboratory experiments published in 2024 found that microplastic particles can increase foam height and stability by accumulating at bubble interfaces and slowing film drainage.5 The experiment does not establish the effect’s magnitude in the open ocean, but suggests that plastic may alter the physical behaviour of the air–water boundary.


Contamination is not necessarily immobilised. Field research has shown that persistent perfluoroalkyl acids – anthropogenic pollutants – can return from seawater to the atmosphere in sea spray.6 A 2025 coastal study likewise identified wastewater-derived chemicals in airborne particles generated near polluted seawater.7 The ocean is not a final sink: deposited substances may be remobilised through another environmental compartment.


This circulation complicates the distinction between natural and polluted foam. Biological surfactants and synthetic particles may accumulate at the same air–water interfaces, while microbial habitats coexist with persistent chemicals. Foam becomes a hybrid formation in which ecological productivity and anthropogenic disturbance cannot be separated by sight. Its beauty offers no evidence of ecological health.



The AI-generated music introduces another unstable synthesis. It does not document the acoustic environment of Belle-Île-en-Mer as a field recording would. Instead, it constructs an atmosphere through computational recombination. Relationships within human-made musical data are converted into a new sequence whose sounds need not have any direct acoustic origin in the place represented on screen.


The soundtrack operates as a second foam. It gathers dispersed cultural material, concentrates statistical relationships and stabilises them temporarily as audible form. Like marine foam, it is generated at an interface: here between datasets, algorithmic prediction and artistic selection. Its continuity conceals prior fragments and probabilities.


This analogy does not equate biological and computational processes. It identifies a shared logic of aggregation. Marine foam depends upon organisms, molecules, waves and currents; the music upon training material, computation, infrastructure and editorial decisions.



The recording, analytical display and soundtrack establish three distinct relationships to representation. The video records the visible behaviour of an event. The display translates that record into measurable colour and brightness. The music produces an artificial atmosphere through generative synthesis. Observation, measurement and construction remain distinct, yet each transforms material received from elsewhere.


Foamscape places visual attention beside scientific knowledge and asks what becomes perceptible when an ephemeral surface is treated as an ecological subject. Foam appears as habitat, biochemical concentrator, transport mechanism and possible index of environmental change.


The work presents foam as an unstable archive: a structure in which biological production, technological representation and anthropogenic contamination become briefly visible before returning to circulation.
